# Why South Florida Summer Is the Worst Time of Year for Indoor Air Quality

July in South Florida isn't just hot — it's the perfect storm for indoor air quality problems. The combination of 90°F+ temperatures, 80%+ humidity, and afternoon thunderstorms creates conditions where indoor pollutants thrive. Here's what happens inside your home during summer and how to fix it.

## The Six-Week Mold Spore Cycle

Here's a pattern we see every summer: A homeowner notices a musty smell in early July, ignores it through August, and by September they're dealing with visible mold growth on AC vents and drywall.

Mold doesn't need much to grow. When indoor humidity stays above 60% (which happens in most South Florida homes without active dehumidification), mold spores land on a surface and germinate within 24-48 hours. The musty smell isn't the problem — it's the **warning**. By the time you smell it, there's already an active colony.

**The trigger point is 60% relative humidity.** Your AC typically removes enough moisture to keep you under that threshold *if* it's running enough. But on milder summer days (low 80s, rainy), the AC cycles less and humidity creeps up. This is why you can smell mustiness on a cloudy 82°F day but not on a 96°F scorcher.

## Pollen Gets Trapped Indoors During Summer

Contrary to what most people think, pollen season doesn't end in spring in South Florida. Different plants release at different times:

| Season | Dominant Allergen |
|--------|------------------|
| Spring (Mar-May) | Oak, birch, grass pollen |
| Summer (Jun-Sep) | Grass, ragweed, mold spores |
| Fall (Oct-Nov) | Ragweed, mold spores |
| Winter (Dec-Feb) | Still active — Florida never truly freezes |

The problem during summer is that people close windows and run AC constantly — which recirculates whatever pollen made it inside. Without proper filtration, that pollen stays trapped.

## The Four-Layer Defense Against Summer IAQ Problems

### Layer 1: Filter Strategy (The most important thing you can do)

Standard 1-inch fiberglass filters (MERV 1-4) catch about 7% of airborne particles. They're basically window screens for your AC. In summer, this is inadequate.

Switch to a **MERV 8 or MERV 11** filter during summer months. This catches 70-85% of pollen, mold spores, and dust. Important: check your system specs first — MERV 13+ can restrict airflow on older units and cause freezing.

Replace every 30 days during summer. Mark your calendar. A dirty filter in July loses efficiency fast because the AC is running more.

### Layer 2: Humidity Control

Your AC is your primary dehumidifier. If it's sized correctly, it removes about 3-4 pints of water per hour of runtime. Problems arise when:

- The system is **oversized** — it cools the house too fast, short-cycles, and doesn't run long enough to remove humidity
- The **condensate drain** is clogged — water backs up, overflows the pan, and creates a breeding ground in your attic/crawlspace
- The **blower speed** is too high — water blows off the coils before it can drain

Signs your humidity control is failing: condensation on windows, musty smell, clammy feeling even at 72°F, water stains near vents.

### Layer 3: Source Control

The most effective IAQ strategy is removing pollutants at the source, not filtering them after they're airborne:

- **Shoes off at the door** — eliminates 60% of tracked-in pollutants (pollen, soil, pesticides)
- **Quick showers after being outside** — washes off pollen before it gets on furniture
- **Seal windows and doors** — check weatherstripping in June, before peak season
- **Don't dry clothes indoors** — adds moisture to indoor air
- **Use kitchen exhaust while cooking** — removes particles and excess humidity at the source
